Resist the Ecosystem

February 26, 2023 · 5 min · 903 words

We arrived in an age where you have to make a choice.

  • Apple or Google?
  • Android or iOS?
  • Windows or macOS?

Either way you are entering not only an operating system but also a whole ecosystem. Every major company strives to provide a whole suite of services and software. If you enter the Apple world you are going to use iCloud, iCloud KeyChain, Apple Mail and so on. Being inside of an ecosystem has advantages. One of them can be security. If all of your eggs are in one basket and that basket has strong security that can be a good thing. Also there is the argument of having less parties to trust. If you decide to use Google services wherever possible you only have one party to trust. But those ecosystems also try to lock you in. Lets say you are deeply into the Apple ecosystem. Thus you are probably using iCloud Keychain to store all of your passwords. iCloud Keychain however only works on iOS, iPadOS, macOS and so on. So using iCloud Keychain on Windows or Android will either not be possible or an unpleasant experience. And this phenomenon happens every time, Apple being the one that locks you in the most aggressively. Ecosystems are nice, I’m not going to deny that. Before we’re looking at the why not we are exploring how to stay out of them.

The first choices to make are the operating systems and hardware choices. My current recommendations would be MicroOS for any desktop device and GrapheneOS on a Google Pixel for a mobile device. There is nothing wrong with using sandboxed Google Play Services and downloading your apps from the Google Play Store, as this also provides better security than F-Droid. So our goal is not necessarily to be completely degoogled. Using the Google Play Store on GrapheneOS does not lure us into the Google ecosystem when compared to the stock Android experience. Furthermore GrapheneOS provides enhanced security when compared to stock Android. I generally recommend avoiding Apple at all cost, I don’t like their vendor lock-in at all.

Now the though part, why do I prefer to stay out of the ecosystem? The obvious answer is that I do so because I can. The point should be made that staying out of the ecosystem requires some amount of effort and being able to make sacrifices, not everyone is able to do that. Then the next question arises if you can stay out, why would you choose to do so. For me personally I just dislike them from the ground up. They want you to subscribe to their services, e.g. Apple One, Google One, Microsoft 365. They integrate everything deeply into their operating system to lure you into their services. I just don’t like that. They have to do that in order to stay profitable and competitive. And while I like that software from big companies sometimes provides greater security, I don’t like to use too much of their software as that lures me into their ecosystem. Occasions where I have chosen software from big companies over free software is Chromium and the Google Play Store.

While the Apple ecosystem is arguably the most expensive one, the Google ecosystem, if existent, is most often thought of as a set of free services like the search engine, Gmail, Google Maps, etc. And I do indeed use some of their services like their search engine, YouTube and Google Maps and they I think they are excellent and I wouldn’t want to miss them. The reason why I’m not investing deeper into the Google ecosystem is diversification. I don’t like making myself dependant on a single company. If there were to happen something to Google I’d have to choose a new search engine, a new maps application, a new website to stream videos and a new place to download my apps from. GrapheneOS and Android are possibly going to survive due to their open source nature.

The point I’m trying to make is, determine which services are really valuable and avoid all other services they provide. Don’t act like big companies don’t exist by degoogling everything at all costs, this may weaken your security unintentionally. Also don’t enter an ecosystem irresponsibly, as this increases your dependency. Depending on a single company for multiple types of information may also be a risk to your privacy, as that company now is a single point of failure. If that company has a security breach or turns out to be not trustworthy, you’re screwed. Having less parties to trust is a double-edged sword.

In the end it’s going to be a personal choice. How much sacrifices are you able to make? What services are absolutely indispensable for you? Depending on your answers, you may not be able to stay out of the ecosystem. Although if you’re not trapped inside of the Apple ecosystem, GrapheneOS is very approachable if you’re willing to use a Google Pixel. Jumping from Windows or macOS to MicroOS is admittedly a bigger step. Switching to MicroOS is potentially the biggest step. But there are a multitude of smaller steps that can be done before which act as a preparation, like switching to an independent email provider or an independent password manager. And for those of you that might be interested in making the switch, there may be more blog posts coming regarding that topic. Just stay tuned.
